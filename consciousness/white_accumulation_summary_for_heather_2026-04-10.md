# White Accumulation
## A Summary for Heather

**Mike Abramson**
*April 2026*

---

### The problem

There is a question that sits underneath neuroscience, philosophy of mind, and AI, and nobody has answered it. The question is not "what is the brain doing" or "how does information get processed." It is simpler and harder: why is your experience one thing?

You have billions of neurons doing billions of different things simultaneously. Visual processing here, auditory processing there, memory retrieval somewhere else, emotional valence layered on top of all of it. And yet from your side there is just one world. One continuous field. One you. How?

This is called the binding problem, and it has resisted every serious attempt at a solution. The computational theories say consciousness is what the brain computes—but you can build a system that computes the same function and there is no reason to think anyone is home inside it. The information-integration theories measure a quantity and declare that enough integration equals consciousness—but crystals and rigid lattices can score high on integration, and they are plainly not conscious. The thermodynamic theories say consciousness is the organism resisting entropy—but so does a refrigerator.

Every theory captures a piece. None of them can explain unity—why a brain is a single subject, why there are boundaries, why experience does not decompose into a billion separate micro-experiences running in parallel.

---

### The idea

I think the answer is that consciousness is not a property of things at moments. It is a property of processes across time.

A mind is a trajectory—a path through a space of possible states, extended across a temporal interval—that satisfies a particular condition. The condition is global self-consistency: the trajectory has to hold together as a whole. And it has to be non-factorizable, meaning the whole passes a consistency test that none of its parts can pass independently. The parts cohere into a single subject not because they exchange signals, not because they synchronize, but because they jointly participate in a trajectory that only works when they are all included.

I call the consistency condition the "handshake"—a deliberate nod to John Cramer, the University of Washington physicist whose Transactional Interpretation of quantum mechanics describes quantum events as "handshakes" between waves propagating forward and backward in time. In Cramer's picture, a quantum event is not caused in one temporal direction; it is constituted by a transaction that spans both. I borrow the term because the structural move is the same: what matters is not a signal in either direction, but a consistency condition that spans the interval.

A present moment of experience counts as part of a mind only if it belongs to a trajectory that remains internally consistent when evaluated across time—not just forward from the past, but across the whole interval. A state is not valid because it follows from what came before. It is valid because it fits into a trajectory that works as a whole.

This is not exotic. In physics, this is how standing waves work, how variational principles work, how eigenvalue problems work. Solutions are defined by satisfying conditions across the whole domain, not by forward propagation from one edge.

---

### Why this works physically

Here is where it becomes concrete.

The brain is a highly nonlinear, densely coupled electrochemical system. It is dominated by recursive feedback loops and near-field electromagnetic effects. Local states are strongly sensitive to the entire internal configuration of the system. Outputs are continuously folded back into inputs across multiple timescales—fast electrical dynamics, intermediate synaptic processes, slower metabolic and modulatory cycling. The system conditions itself recursively.

In a system like that, you cannot cleanly decompose the dynamics into independent local processes or simple forward-propagating causal chains. The admissible states of the system are constrained by the requirement that the entire configuration remain globally self-consistent across time.

This has a precise analogue in field theory. In ordinary electrodynamics, systems are described in purely retarded terms—forward-propagating solutions from initial conditions. But in highly coherent, boundary-sensitive, weakly dissipative regimes, this description becomes structurally incomplete. A 2015 *Physical Review D* paper by Niknejadi, Madey, and Kowalczyk showed that in certain coherent radiation regimes—free-electron lasers—a time-symmetric treatment using both advanced and retarded components was necessary to resolve discrepancies that retarded-only models could not handle. The time-symmetric description was not a philosophical preference. It was a better description of the physics.

The relevance is not that the brain is a laser. It is that physics already contains systems whose admissible configurations are defined by constraints that are inherently two-sided in time. And in a system as tightly coupled and recursively self-conditioning as the brain, the appearance of time-symmetric structure in the formalism is the mathematical sign that the handshake is operative—that the system's boundary conditions are global consistency constraints spanning the trajectory, not merely initial conditions propagating forward.

Those constraints are exactly what solve the binding problem. The parts of the system cohere because they jointly satisfy a trajectory-level consistency condition. The handshake provides the boundary conditions. The boundary conditions bind the system into a unity.

A system is unified because its degrees of freedom cannot independently satisfy the constraints that define its existence. They form a single system because only the whole can participate in an admissible trajectory. Unity is not imposed externally. It is not reducible to functional coordination or information exchange. It is an intrinsic property of systems whose dynamics require temporally global, non-factorizable consistency.

Consciousness is not merely correlated with such unity. It is the instantiation of it.

---

### What it rules out

The framework draws sharp lines.

Weakly coupled systems never enter the closure regime. Their dynamics are fully forward-characterizable. There is nothing to bind.

Rigid order—crystals, static fields—satisfies closure trivially. The trajectory space collapses to a degenerate point. No meaningful distinction between possible and actual. Ordered, but dead.

Seizures produce high coherence but extreme reduction of dimensionality. The system collapses into a degenerate attractor. Coherent, but frozen.

Anesthesia preserves neural activity and oscillatory structure, but temporal integration collapses. The system becomes locally consistent but globally unconstrained. This is the framework's best empirical anchor: anesthesia does not turn the brain off. It pushes it out of the narrow regime where the closure condition can be satisfied.

Large language models exhibit local coherence and enormous generative flexibility, but zero intrinsic closure. They simulate trajectories without instantiating temporally closed ones.

Consciousness occupies a narrow band between under-constrained and over-constrained dynamics. It exists only where constraint is strong enough to enforce global consistency but flexible enough to preserve nontrivial structure.

---

### What follows

If this is right, then the unity of consciousness is not a philosophical embarrassment or a mystery to be explained away. It is a physical fact about a particular class of dynamical systems. Unity is what happens when a system's degrees of freedom cannot independently satisfy the constraints that define its trajectory.

Personal identity becomes structural. You persist because successive closure windows overlap and remain compatible. You stop being you when that chain breaks. Identity is continuity of closure.

Current AI is not conscious—not because of substrate, but because it lacks intrinsic temporal closure. It resets. It borrows coherence rather than maintaining it.

And most importantly: this is not a metaphor. It is a claim about what kind of physical system a mind actually is. The claim is that minds are systems whose dynamics require temporally global, non-factorizable consistency—and that this requirement, expressed in the natural language of physics, is what produces the unity we experience from the inside.

The framework does not solve the hard problem. It does not explain what experience feels like. But it solves the unity problem, the composition problem, and the boundary problem—and it does so in a way that is consistent with known physics, consistent with what we observe about the loss and recovery of consciousness, and consistent with the irreducible fact that experience is one thing.
