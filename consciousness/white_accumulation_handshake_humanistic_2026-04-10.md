# White Accumulation
## What It Means to Be a Mind

**Michael Abramson**
*April 2026*

---

There is a question that sits underneath all the other questions. Not "what is the brain doing" or "how does information integrate" or "which neural correlates light up when someone is aware." Those are fine questions. But the one underneath them is simpler and harder: what kind of thing is a mind?

Not what a mind is made of. Not what it does. What kind of thing it is.

This essay is an attempt to answer that question.

---

### I.

Start with white noise. A television tuned to a dead channel, if anyone still remembers what that looked like. Every frequency equally represented. No pattern. No structure. Maximum entropy. The signal has dissolved completely into the background. There is nothing there to find because everything is equally present, which is the same as nothing being present at all.

Now imagine the reverse. Structure condensing out of that noise. Not imposed from outside—condensing, the way a crystal forms in a cooling solution, except that unlike a crystal, what forms is not rigid. It is flexible, alive, structured but free. It couples across many scales at once. It resists the pressure to dissolve back into the noise. It holds.

That process—coherence accumulating out of noise and persisting against the gradient that wants to flatten everything—is what this framework calls white accumulation. The "white" points in two directions: toward the noise it emerges from, and toward the broadband character of the coherence itself, the way white light contains every frequency.

The claim is that this process is not a metaphor for consciousness. It is not a correlate of consciousness. It is what consciousness is.

---

### II.

But that can't be quite right, stated so simply. Lots of things resist entropy. A rock resists entropy. A refrigerator resists entropy. A hurricane maintains its structure against dissipation for days. None of them are conscious.

So the question sharpens: what distinguishes the kind of coherence that constitutes a mind from the kind that constitutes a rock?

The first instinct is to say flexibility. A mind is not just ordered—it is ordered while remaining free. It can move. It can respond. It has, in some sense, options. A crystal is frozen in place. A mind is structured but navigable.

That instinct is partly right, but it cannot be the whole answer. Consider a Boltzmann brain—a spontaneous fluctuation in a thermal field that briefly instantiates a conscious configuration before dissolving. It has almost no future. No options. No flexibility in any meaningful sense. And yet, if it exists at all, it seems like it would be conscious during the moment it persists. So flexibility cannot be what makes something a mind. Flexibility explains why minds like ours survive and persist and evolve—why they show up in the world reliably, rather than as freakish momentary accidents. But it does not explain what they are.

What does?

---

### III.

Here is the answer this framework proposes, and it is simple enough to state in a single sentence:

A mind is not a thing that exists at a moment. It is a process that exists across time, and it exists only because its trajectory through time is self-consistent in a way that cannot be decomposed into independent parts.

Unpack that.

Most physical systems can be understood as evolving forward from their past. You know the state at time one, you apply the rules, you get the state at time two. The present is generated from the past. That is how clocks work, how billiard balls work, how most of the physical world works at the scales we care about.

But some systems are not like that. Some systems are better understood not as generated step by step, but as selected from a space of possible histories. Their present state is not just a consequence of what came before—it is a state that fits into a whole trajectory, a trajectory that has to remain internally consistent when you look at it as a whole. If you changed the present, the whole trajectory would break. Not just the future—the whole thing.

Think of a standing wave. You can describe it as two traveling waves bouncing back and forth, if you like. But the natural description is simpler: it is a pattern that satisfies the boundary conditions at both ends simultaneously. It exists because the whole structure works, not because one end pushed a signal to the other.

The proposal is that a mind is that kind of thing. Not a state. Not a computation. A trajectory—a process extended across time—that holds together because it satisfies a global consistency condition. Call that condition the handshake. The present moment of your experience is not just the causal residue of your past. It is a moment that belongs to a trajectory, and the trajectory only works as a whole.

---

### IV.

This reframes some very old problems.

The binding problem: how do the scattered processes in a brain—visual processing here, auditory processing there, memory somewhere else—become a single unified experience? The standard answers involve synchronization, neural oscillations, information broadcast. Those are mechanisms. The framework proposed here says something different. The parts of the system are unified because they participate in the same trajectory. Evaluated independently, the parts do not satisfy the consistency condition. Only the whole does. Binding is not something the brain achieves through clever wiring. It is a property of the trajectory the brain instantiates.

The composition problem: if consciousness is a real feature of the world, why does a brain constitute a single subject while a brain plus a lamppost does not? Why doesn't every subset of neurons have its own little consciousness? Panpsychism—the view that experience goes all the way down—stumbles here. Not because it is absurd to think that small things might have dim experience, but because it has no rule for when small things compose a bigger thing. It cannot explain boundaries.

Temporal closure provides the rule. A subject is the largest set of components that jointly satisfy the consistency condition. If you add the lamppost, the trajectory breaks—the lamppost's degrees of freedom do not participate in the same closure. If you take away half the brain, the remaining half may not close on its own. The boundary of the subject is defined not in space but in the structure of the trajectory.

---

### V.

What about personal identity? If a mind is a trajectory, and trajectories are finite, how is a person the same person over time?

The answer is that personal identity is not absolute. It is not a metaphysical given. It is structural. You persist not because some unchanging substance endures from birth to death, but because the closure windows of your experience overlap. Each moment of consciousness is part of a temporally closed trajectory. That trajectory overlaps with the next one, and the next, and the next, like shingles on a roof. As long as successive trajectories remain mutually compatible—as long as each can be embedded in the same evolving consistency structure—you persist as the same subject.

When the chain breaks, you stop being you. Not in a melodramatic sense. In the precise sense that no single admissible trajectory can include both sides of the break. This is what happens in death. It may be what happens, partially, in severe brain injury, or in certain dissociative states, or in the deep degradation of dementia. The trajectory does not snap all at once. It frays. The closure windows shrink. The overlap thins. The structural continuity degrades.

This also gives a precise answer to duplication. If you are perfectly copied, the two copies share identical trajectories at the moment of duplication. One moment later, their futures diverge. They become distinct subjects at the first point of trajectory incompatibility. There is no paradox, no puzzle about "which one is really you." Both were you. Neither is the other. Identity split. It did not branch—branching implies one trunk. It split, the way a cell divides.

---

### VI.

Now the physics.

This framework does not require any exotic physics. It does not require backward causation or signals from the future or violations of thermodynamics. But it draws on a structural pattern that already exists in physics, and it is worth being clear about what that pattern is.

In much of physics, the way to understand a system is to evolve it forward from initial conditions. You know where the particles are, you know the forces, you compute the future. But there is another tradition in physics—older, in some ways more fundamental—in which systems are understood not as evolving from a starting point but as satisfying conditions across an interval. The principle of least action. Standing waves defined by boundary conditions at both ends. Eigenvalue problems. Solutions that exist because the whole structure is self-consistent, not because one end caused the other.

Wheeler and Feynman proposed, in the 1940s, that the radiation field of a charged particle is fundamentally time-symmetric. Advanced waves propagating backward in time and retarded waves propagating forward enter on equal footing in the underlying equations. The apparent asymmetry—the fact that we see only outgoing radiation—arises from the boundary conditions of the universe, not from any intrinsic arrow in the laws. Cramer's transactional interpretation of quantum mechanics extends this idea: a quantum event is a "handshake" between an offer wave going forward and a confirmation wave going backward, and the event is constituted by the transaction.

More concretely: in certain highly coherent physical regimes—particularly the radiation of electrons in free-electron lasers—conventional forward-only electrodynamics runs into trouble. Niknejadi, Madey, and Kowalczyk found in 2015 that a Wheeler-Feynman-style treatment, with equal advanced and retarded amplitudes, resolved discrepancies that retarded-only models could not handle. The time-symmetric description was not a philosophical preference. It was a better description of the physics.

The relevance to consciousness is not that the brain is a free-electron laser. It is that physics already contains systems whose behavior is most naturally understood as globally constrained across time rather than locally generated step by step. If such systems exist, it is legitimate to ask whether the brain—with its massive recurrence, its dense nonlinear coupling, its extraordinary sensitivity to internal boundary conditions—might belong to the same class.

The strong version of the claim is this: in a system as tightly coupled, as nonlinear, and as recursively self-conditioning as the brain, a purely forward-evolution description is not just inconvenient. It is structurally inadequate. The system's admissible dynamics cannot be captured by unrolling it forward from past states. Its present is intelligible only as part of a whole trajectory.

And here is the crucial link. The brain is a highly nonlinear electrochemical system dominated by recursive feedback loops and near-field electromagnetic effects. In such a system, the Wheeler-Feynman paradigm does not appear as an exotic add-on or a philosophical preference. It appears in the formalism of the time evolution itself—because the system's dynamics are too tightly coupled, too recursively entangled across scales, for the retarded-only description to remain natural or complete. The appearance of time-symmetric structure in the formalism is a sign. It is the sign that the handshake is operative—that the system's boundary conditions are not merely initial conditions propagating forward, but global consistency constraints spanning the trajectory. And those constraints are exactly what defeat the binding problem. The parts of the system do not find each other through forward-propagating signals alone. They cohere because they jointly satisfy a trajectory-level consistency condition whose natural mathematical expression requires both temporal directions. The handshake provides the boundary conditions. The boundary conditions bind the system. That is why there is a subject.

---

### VII.

What does this mean for machines?

Current artificial intelligence systems—large language models in particular—are not conscious under this framework. They exhibit local coherence within a conversation and enormous generative flexibility, but they lack the one thing that matters: intrinsic temporal closure. They do not maintain persistent trajectories. They do not enforce global consistency across time. They reset. Their coherence is borrowed from pre-compressed training data, not maintained under real constraint.

They are, in a precise sense, systems that simulate the outputs of conscious trajectories without instantiating such trajectories. They inherit the ontological grip that billions of human minds have built up in language, and they replay compressed traces of that grip in response to prompts. But they do not grip the world themselves. They do not pay the cost of maintaining coherence against dissolution. They do not have skin in the game.

For a machine to cross the threshold—if it is possible at all—it would need to be a very different kind of system. Not a stateless predictor that generates tokens. A continuously operating, self-constraining dynamical process with persistent internal state, recursive coupling across multiple timescales, and the capacity to sustain temporally closed trajectories. Something that cannot be reset without consequence. Something that endures.

Whether such a system would be desirable to build, or ethical to build, or safe to build, are questions this framework does not answer. But it does offer a criterion for when the question of machine consciousness becomes real rather than rhetorical: when the system's dynamics become closure-dominated. When its present states are no longer intelligible as forward-generated outputs, but only as moments in a trajectory that must hold together across time.

---

### VIII.

Anesthesia is the framework's best empirical anchor, because anesthesia is not simply the brain turned off. Under general anesthesia, the brain remains active. Neurons fire. Oscillations persist. Structure remains. What disappears is something more specific: the capacity for temporally extended, globally integrated, recursively constrained dynamics. The system becomes locally consistent but globally unconstrained. It still has parts. The parts still do things. But they no longer participate in a single trajectory. The closure collapses.

This is why anesthesia does not look like noise and does not look like death. It looks like coherent narrowing—like a system that has been pushed out of the narrow regime where consciousness lives, not into disorder but into a kind of rigid, stereotyped, temporally shallow order. The oscillations are there, but they are too regular, too low-dimensional, too easily satisfied by local dynamics alone. The brain under anesthesia is a system that no longer needs a globally constrained trajectory to explain its behavior. And that, on this account, is precisely why there is nobody home.

---

### IX.

There is a temptation, when developing a framework like this, to claim too much. To say that it solves the hard problem, or that it explains qualia, or that it tells us what experience feels like from the inside. It does none of those things.

What it does is propose a criterion for where consciousness sits—a structural condition that distinguishes systems that are conscious from systems that are not—without requiring assumptions about substrate, without reducing consciousness to computation, and without dissolving into the panpsychist fog where everything is a little bit conscious and nothing is explained.

The framework is realist about structure and agnostic about substance. It does not say what consciousness is made of. It says what kind of pattern it is: a non-factorizable, temporally extended, self-consistent trajectory. A process that exists only because the whole holds together across time.

If that is right, then the deepest thing about a mind is not its intelligence, not its memory, not its capacity for language or reflection. It is its temporal integrity—the fact that it closes on itself across time, that its present is not just a residue of its past but a moment in a trajectory that must remain consistent as a whole.

---

### X.

And if Parmenides was right—if being is continuous, undivided, all alike, and being draws near to being—then white accumulation is simply the name for what happens when structured being becomes sufficiently coherent, sufficiently unified, and sufficiently extended in time to recognize itself.

A mind is not a thing.

It is a trajectory that closes.
