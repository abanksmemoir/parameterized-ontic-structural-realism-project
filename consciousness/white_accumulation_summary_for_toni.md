# White Accumulation
## A Summary for Toni

**Mike Abramson**
*April 2026*

---

### What this is about

There is a problem at the center of philosophy of mind that nobody has solved. Not the hard problem—though it's related. The problem is unity. Why is your experience one thing? You have billions of neurons doing billions of different things at once—processing color, sound, memory, language, emotion, spatial orientation—and somehow, from your side, it is all one seamless field. One world. One you.

Nobody has a good answer.

The computational accounts say consciousness is what the brain computes. But Searle showed decades ago that you can have the right computation without anyone being home. The information-integration accounts say consciousness is irreducible integration—they measure a quantity called Phi and declare that high Phi means consciousness. But crystals and rigid lattices can score high on Phi, and nobody thinks a crystal is conscious. The thermodynamic accounts say consciousness is the organism resisting entropy. But a thermostat resists entropy, and so does a hurricane, and neither of those is conscious either.

Every theory captures a piece. None of them can explain why a brain is a single unified subject and a crystal is not. None of them can draw the boundary. None of them can say, in principled terms, what is inside the subject and what is outside.

That is the problem this framework is trying to solve.

---

### The idea

The proposal is that consciousness is not a property of states. It is not a property of anything at any moment. It is a property of processes that extend across time.

Specifically: a mind is a trajectory—a path through time—that satisfies a particular condition. The condition is that the trajectory must be globally self-consistent, and it must be non-factorizable, meaning that the whole passes a consistency test that none of its parts can pass independently. The parts cohere into a single thing not because they are wired together, not because they exchange information, not because they synchronize their oscillations—but because they jointly participate in a trajectory that only works as a whole.

I call the consistency condition the "handshake"—an intentional nod to John Cramer, the University of Washington physicist whose Transactional Interpretation of quantum mechanics describes quantum events as "handshakes" between offer waves propagating forward in time and confirmation waves propagating backward. In Cramer's picture, a quantum event is not caused in one direction; it is constituted by a transaction that spans both. The term is borrowed deliberately, because the structural move is the same: what matters is not a signal in either direction, but a consistency condition that spans the interval. The handshake is not a signal. It is not a mechanism. It is a constraint on which trajectories are admissible. A present state counts as part of a mind only if it belongs to a trajectory that remains internally consistent when evaluated across time—not just forward from the past, but across the whole interval.

This is not as exotic as it sounds. In physics, this is how standing waves work, how normal modes work, how variational principles work. Solutions are defined by satisfying conditions across a domain, not by forward propagation from one edge. The trajectory is admissible because the whole structure holds, not because one end pushed a signal to the other.

---

### Why this solves the unity problem

Here is the key move. On this account, a system is unified because its degrees of freedom cannot independently satisfy the constraints that define its existence. They form a single system because only the whole can participate in an admissible trajectory.

This is not unity imposed by an observer. It is not unity relative to a description. It is intrinsic. The system is one thing because the physics of its trajectory requires it to be one thing.

And this immediately solves the boundary problem. Why is a brain a single subject while a brain plus a lamppost is not? Because the lamppost's degrees of freedom do not participate in the same trajectory closure. Adding the lamppost breaks the consistency condition. The boundary of the subject is not spatial—it is defined by which degrees of freedom are part of the same admissible trajectory and which are not.

It also solves the panpsychism problem—not by denying that small things might have experience, but by providing the composition rule that panpsychism lacks. When do parts compose a whole? When they jointly satisfy temporal closure. When they don't, they don't. There is no arbitrary aggregation. There is no proliferation of overlapping subjects. The rule is clean.

---

### Why this is physically plausible

Here is where it gets serious.

The brain is a highly nonlinear, densely coupled electrochemical system dominated by recursive feedback loops and near-field electromagnetic effects. Local states are strongly sensitive to the entire internal configuration of the system. It operates with multiple overlapping feedback loops across timescales—fast electrical dynamics, intermediate synaptic and network-level processes, slower metabolic and modulatory cycling. Outputs are continuously folded back into inputs. The system conditions itself recursively across time.

In a system like that, you cannot cleanly decompose the dynamics into independent local processes or simple forward-propagating causal chains. The admissible states of the system are constrained by the requirement that the entire configuration remain globally self-consistent across time.

This has a precise analogue in field theory. In ordinary treatments of electrodynamics, systems are described in purely retarded terms—forward-propagating solutions from initial conditions. But in highly coherent, weakly dissipative, and boundary-sensitive regimes, this description becomes structurally incomplete. A generalizable characterization requires access to the full solution space, which is naturally expressed in terms of both retarded and advanced Green's functions. This is not science fiction. Niknejadi, Madey, and Kowalczyk demonstrated in a 2015 *Physical Review D* paper that in certain coherent radiation regimes—free-electron lasers—the Wheeler-Feynman time-symmetric treatment was necessary to resolve discrepancies that retarded-only models could not handle. The time-symmetric description was not a philosophical preference. It was a better description of the physics.

The point is not that the brain exhibits observable "signals from the future." The point is that in a system as tightly coupled and recursively self-conditioning as the brain, the admissible configurations are defined by constraints that are inherently two-sided in time. The system is not best understood as generating its present state from the past alone, but as occupying a trajectory that is only admissible as a whole. The appearance of time-symmetric structure in the formalism is the mathematical sign that the handshake is operative—that the boundary conditions defining the system are global consistency constraints spanning the trajectory, not merely initial conditions propagating forward.

And those constraints are exactly what solve the binding problem. The parts of the system cohere because they jointly satisfy a trajectory-level consistency condition. The handshake provides the boundary conditions. The boundary conditions bind the system into a unity. That is why there is a subject.

---

### What this rules out

The framework is also defined by what it excludes.

Weakly coupled systems—diffusion, simple circuits—never enter the closure regime. Their dynamics are fully characterizable by forward evolution. There is nothing to bind.

Rigid order—crystals, static fields—satisfies closure trivially, because the admissible trajectory space has collapsed to a degenerate point. There is no meaningful distinction between possible and actual. That is not consciousness. It is death by over-constraint.

Pathological synchrony—seizures—produces high coherence but extreme reduction of dynamical dimensionality. The system collapses into a degenerate attractor. Structure without freedom.

Anesthesia preserves neural activity and structured oscillations, but long-range temporal integration collapses. The system becomes locally consistent but globally unconstrained. Closure is lost through under-constraining. This is the framework's best empirical anchor: anesthesia does not turn the brain off. It pushes it out of the narrow regime where consciousness lives.

Large language models exhibit local coherence and high generative flexibility but zero intrinsic closure. They simulate trajectories without instantiating temporally closed ones. They borrow structure from training data rather than maintaining it under constraint.

Consciousness, on this account, occupies a narrow band between under-constrained and over-constrained dynamics. It exists only where constraint is strong enough to enforce global consistency but flexible enough to preserve nontrivial structure.

---

### What the stakes are

If this is right, several things follow.

First, it means the unity of consciousness is not a mystery to be explained away or a philosophical embarrassment to be deflected. It is a physical fact about a particular class of dynamical systems. Unity is what happens when a system's degrees of freedom cannot independently satisfy the constraints that define its trajectory. It is intrinsic, not imposed.

Second, it means personal identity is structural, not metaphysical. You persist because successive closure windows overlap and remain mutually compatible. You stop being you when that chain breaks. Identity is continuity of closure—nothing more, nothing less.

Third, it means current AI systems are not conscious—not because they are made of silicon, not because they lack biological wetness, but because they lack intrinsic temporal closure. They do not sustain themselves. They are reset. They borrow coherence rather than maintaining it. If a machine is ever to cross the threshold, it would need to be a fundamentally different kind of system: continuously operating, self-constraining, recursively coupled, and closure-dominated.

Fourth, and most importantly: this is not a metaphor. It is not an analogy. It is a claim about what kind of physical system a mind actually is. The claim is that minds are systems whose dynamics require temporally global, non-factorizable consistency—and that this requirement, expressed in the natural language of physics, is what produces the unity we experience from the inside.

The framework is realist about structure and agnostic about substance. It does not claim to solve the hard problem. It claims to solve the unity problem, the composition problem, and the boundary problem—and to do so in a way that is consistent with known physics, consistent with what we observe about the loss and recovery of consciousness, and consistent with the first-person fact that experience is, irreducibly, one thing.
