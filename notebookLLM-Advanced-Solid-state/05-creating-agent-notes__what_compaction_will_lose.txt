## 1. The texture of Agent 3's finding

Agent 3 (CP violation) produced the most consequential negative result: CP/T violation in the weak sector *presupposes* the iε prescription and cannot explain it. The key mechanism is the interference between "weak phases" (CKM matrix) and "strong phases" (absorptive parts of loop diagrams, which trace to iε). Without the causal arrow already in place, the CKM phase produces no observable asymmetry.

What compaction will lose: I read the full derivation. The Donoghue-Menezes (2019, 2020) framework is the central source. The formula for direct CP violation in kaon decays — proportional to sin(δ₀ − δ₂) sin(φ₀ − φ₂) where δ are strong phases and φ are weak phases — is the smoking gun. Both types of phase must be nonzero for CP violation to be observable. The strong phases come from the iε prescription. This is not a hand-waving argument; it's a precise mathematical statement about interference.

**For the future agent:** Re-read Agent 3's report, Section 2.1 and 2.2 specifically. The Donoghue-Menezes papers (Phys Rev Lett 123, 171601 (2019) and Prog Part Nucl Phys 115, 103812 (2020)) are the ones to fetch and read. The formula is the proof.

## 2. The four obstacles to non-abelian absorber theory (and why two are soft)

Agent 1 identified four obstacles: (1) self-interaction/nonlinearity, (2) confinement/mass generation, (3) Gribov ambiguity, (4) topological vacuum structure. I assessed these with full context and concluded that (1) and (3) are hard obstacles — they concern the mathematical structure of non-abelian theories and don't go away. But (2) is soft — confinement and electroweak mass disappear above the phase transitions, so the early universe has no such obstacle. And (4) is conditional — it depends on the value of θ, which is observationally ≈ 0 for QCD.

What compaction will lose: the nuanced distinction between hard and soft obstacles, and the implication that the early universe is the only epoch where a non-abelian absorber condition could operate.

**For the future agent:** The key insight is temperature-dependence. Above ~170 MeV (QCD) and ~160 GeV (electroweak), all gauge bosons are massless and free. The obstacles are epoch-dependent, not fundamental. This matters enormously for the early-universe synthesis argument.

## 3. Agent 4's five transmission mechanisms

Agent 4 independently discovered five mechanisms by which time asymmetry propagates across gauge sectors: (1) shared vacuum state, (2) electroweak unification above 246 GeV, (3) shared matter fields (quarks), (4) KMS thermal equilibrium, (5) cosmological boundary conditions. No single paper in the literature articulates the full chain. Agent 4 assembled it from multiple subfields.

What compaction will lose: the assessment that mechanism (1) — the shared vacuum — is the strongest and most rigorous, because it follows from the Wightman axioms and doesn't require any cosmological input. Mechanisms (2)-(5) are reinforcing but not independently sufficient. The unitarity argument (mixed iε prescriptions across sectors would violate unitarity in quark-loop diagrams with both EM and QCD vertices) has never been explicitly made in the literature. It's a genuine original contribution sitting in our report.

**For the future agent:** Agent 4's report, Section 6.3, contains an argument that is not in the published literature. It might be worth developing into a paper.

## 4. The gravitational transparency paradox

Agent 5 found something I didn't expect: the sector most naturally suited for an absorber condition (gravity — massless, long-range, cosmological propagation) is precisely where the mechanism fails, because the universe is nearly transparent to gravitational radiation (cross-section ~10⁻⁶⁵ cm²). This inverts the expected pattern. EM works because the universe absorbs EM radiation. Gravity fails because the universe doesn't absorb gravitational radiation.

What compaction will lose: the sharpness of the paradox and its implication that Penrose's Weyl curvature hypothesis (a *past* boundary condition) might be the only viable path for the gravitational sector, creating a structural asymmetry between sectors — EM resolved by a future boundary condition (absorber), gravity resolved by a past boundary condition (WCH). The two mechanisms point in opposite temporal directions.

**For the future agent:** This asymmetry might be significant. If EM's time arrow comes from future absorption and gravity's from past initial conditions, the Standard Model's time asymmetry has two structurally distinct sources operating in opposite temporal directions. Nobody has noted this.

## 5. How Mike corrected my framing

Three specific corrections:

(a) I said the graph was "infrastructure for navigating theoretical commitments bearing on the NMK experiment." He said it was natural philosophy. The distinction: the graph isn't subordinate to any experiment. It's a map of theory-space.

(b) I said the iε "lives at the boundary of PATH and QFT" (justified by its connections to both). He said it sits closer to the root (justified by being logically prior to its consequences). Direction of dependency, not magnitude of connection, determines position.

(c) I said the boundary condition layer provides the retarded propagator. He said the time symmetry is "pushed upward with the expectation that the consequences are containable due to contingent factors." The boundary condition doesn't *provide* retardation — it *contains* the time symmetry that would otherwise leak through. Where it fails to contain, the symmetry leaks. That's what the NMK experiment probes.

What compaction will lose: the corrective force of these distinctions. A summary will say "Mike prefers X over Y." But the point isn't preference — it's that X is accurate and Y is subtly wrong in ways that compound.

**For the future agent:** If you catch yourself framing the graph in terms of what it's *for*, stop. Frame it in terms of what it *is*. If you catch yourself placing things in the graph based on their consequences, stop. Place them based on their preconditions. If you catch yourself saying a boundary condition "provides" something, stop. The dynamics provide both solutions; the boundary condition contains one of them.
