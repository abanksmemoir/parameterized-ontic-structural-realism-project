# Agent 3 Report: CP Violation, T Violation, and the Propagator

## Executive Summary

The weak interaction violates CP symmetry through the complex phase in the CKM matrix, and by the CPT theorem this implies T violation -- actual physical processes that distinguish past from future at the dynamical level. The conventional time asymmetry encoded in the propagator choice (the Feynman iε prescription) is a separate, logically independent source of temporal directionality. The literature establishes clearly that these two forms of time asymmetry are **related but not equivalent**: dynamical T violation *presupposes* the causal arrow encoded in the propagator, and observable CP-violating effects arise specifically from the *interference* between T-odd weak phases and the causal phases generated by the iε prescription. However, CP violation does **not** do the structural work that the Wheeler-Feynman absorber condition does for electromagnetism -- it does not explain *why* the retarded propagator is selected over the advanced one in the weak sector. The propagator selection problem in the SU(2) and SU(3) sectors remains unresolved and structurally unaddressed.

---

## 1. Two Distinct Sources of Time Asymmetry in QFT

### 1.1 The Propagator's Causal Arrow (Kinematic/Boundary Condition)

The Feynman propagator encodes a temporal directionality through the iε prescription:

$$iD_F(q) = \frac{i}{q^2 - m^2 + i\varepsilon}$$

This prescription determines pole positions in the complex energy plane and ensures that positive-energy states propagate forward in time. As Donoghue and Menezes argue in their foundational papers (Phys. Rev. Lett. 123, 171601 (2019); Prog. Part. Nucl. Phys. 115, 103812 (2020)), this encodes an intrinsic **arrow of causality** -- "the time direction that causal quantum processes occur." They trace this asymmetry to the factor of *i* in the quantization procedure itself: the choice of exp[+iS] over exp[-iS] in the path integral, or equivalently [x, p] = +iℏ versus -iℏ.

Critically, the Feynman propagator decomposes in coordinate space as:

$$iD_F(x) = D_F^{for}(x)\theta(t) + D_F^{back}(x)\theta(-t)$$

where the forward component propagates positive energy forward in time as e^{-iE_q t}. Reversing the sign of iε yields the time-reversed propagator D_{-F}, where positive energies flow backward. Both choices are internally consistent, but within a given quantization convention, the causal direction is fixed.

This is the same type of time asymmetry that the Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory addresses for electromagnetism: the classical Maxwell equations are time-symmetric, admitting both retarded and advanced solutions. The absorber condition provides a cosmological boundary condition that structurally selects the retarded solution. In QFT, the analogous selection is made by hand through the iε prescription.

### 1.2 Dynamical T Violation (from the Lagrangian)

CP violation in the Standard Model arises from the irreducible complex phase in the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix. For three generations of quarks, one complex phase cannot be removed by field redefinitions; this is the Kobayashi-Maskawa phase. By the CPT theorem -- proved by Jost (1958) in the Wightman axiomatic framework, and by Luders and Pauli in the Lagrangian formulation -- any CP violation implies T violation.

This dynamical T violation means that certain physical processes proceed at different rates depending on the direction of time. The paradigmatic examples are:
- **Neutral kaon oscillations**: CPLEAR demonstrated that P(K^0 → K̄^0) ≠ P(K̄^0 → K^0), the Kabir asymmetry
- **B meson transitions**: BaBar (Phys. Rev. Lett. 109, 211801 (2012)) directly observed T violation by comparing entangled B^0 transitions in opposite temporal directions, using the Banuls-Bernabeu method

This is genuinely dynamical: the Lagrangian itself, through the CKM phase, introduces terms that are not invariant under time reversal.

## 2. The Relationship: Interference, Not Equivalence

### 2.1 The Donoghue-Menezes Analysis

The most direct treatment of how these two time asymmetries relate appears in Donoghue and Menezes (2020). Their key insight is that observable T-violating effects arise from the **interference** between T-odd phases from the Lagrangian (weak phases) and the causal phases generated by the iε prescription (strong phases / final-state interaction phases).

For kaon decays, the amplitude structure illustrates this precisely:

$$A(K^0 \to \pi^+\pi^-) = A_0 e^{i\delta_0} e^{i\phi_0} + A_2 e^{i\delta_2} e^{i\phi_2}$$

where:
- δ_i are **strong phases** (CP-even, arising from final-state interactions / absorptive parts of propagators -- directly tied to the iε prescription)
- φ_i are **weak phases** (CP-odd, arising from the CKM matrix)

The CP-violating rate asymmetry is proportional to:

$$\propto A_0 A_2 \sin(\delta_0 - \delta_2) \sin(\phi_0 - \phi_2)$$

**This formula encodes the central finding**: observable CP violation requires both different weak phases (dynamical T violation from the Lagrangian) AND different strong phases (which arise from the causal direction built into the propagator). If the strong phases δ_i were zero -- equivalently, if there were no causal arrow from the iε prescription -- direct CP violation would be unobservable even with maximal CKM phases.

### 2.2 Strong Phases as the Causal Arrow's Fingerprint

The distinction between "weak phases" and "strong phases" in the CP violation literature maps precisely onto the distinction between the two time asymmetries:

- **Weak phases** (CP-odd): Change sign under CP conjugation. Arise from complex CKM couplings. Represent genuine dynamical T violation in the Lagrangian.

- **Strong phases** (CP-even): Same sign under CP conjugation. Arise from absorptive parts of Feynman loop diagrams (computed via Cutkosky cuts), or equivalently from the imaginary parts of Breit-Wigner propagators for on-shell intermediate states. These phases trace back directly to the factors of iε in propagators -- i.e., to the causal arrow.

As Donoghue and Menezes put it: "T-violating phases from the Lagrangian will in general give a phase difference to the two reactions, with one carrying some phase e^{+iφ(t)} and the other the phase e^{-iφ(t)}. These phases interfere with the e^{-iEt} factor that arises because both reactions describe positive energy states propagating forward in time."

This means **dynamical T violation is observable only in the presence of the causal arrow**. The two asymmetries are not independent: the propagator's causal direction provides the "reference frame" against which the Lagrangian's T-odd phases become physically detectable.

### 2.3 The Asymmetry is One-Way

However, the converse does not hold. The causal arrow (propagator choice) does not require dynamical T violation. A theory with a real CKM matrix (no CP violation) would still have a perfectly well-defined Feynman propagator with the same iε prescription and the same causal arrow. The iε prescription is universal across all gauge sectors and is independent of whether those sectors violate CP.

## 3. Does CP Violation Do the Structural Work of the Absorber Condition?

### 3.1 The Wheeler-Feynman Mechanism

In the absorber theory, the retarded propagator for electromagnetism is selected because photons are massless and long-range, enabling them to propagate to cosmological distances where the universe's future absorbers cancel the advanced component. The mechanism requires:
- Massless, long-range force carriers (photons)
- A cosmological absorber (sufficient future matter to absorb all radiation)
- The resulting boundary condition that selects retarded over advanced solutions

### 3.2 Why CP Violation Cannot Play This Role

CP violation in the weak sector **cannot** serve as an analog of the absorber condition for three fundamental reasons:

**First, it operates at the wrong level.** The absorber condition is a *boundary condition* that selects between two equally valid solutions (retarded and advanced) of time-symmetric equations. CP violation is a property of the *dynamical laws themselves* -- it modifies the Lagrangian, not the boundary conditions. It tells us that certain processes distinguish past from future, but it does not tell us why the retarded propagator was chosen in the first place.

**Second, it presupposes what needs explaining.** As shown in Section 2, observable CP violation requires pre-existing strong phases from the iε prescription. The dynamical T violation in the CKM matrix can only manifest physically because the propagator already has a causal direction. CP violation cannot explain the propagator choice because it is only observable given that choice.

**Third, the physical mechanism doesn't transfer.** The absorber condition works because photons travel to cosmological distances and interact with distant matter. The W and Z bosons of the weak interaction are massive (m_W ≈ 80 GeV, m_Z ≈ 91 GeV), giving them an extremely short range (~10^{-18} m). They cannot propagate to cosmological distances. There is no "absorber" at the far end of a weak interaction. The mechanism that works for U(1) electromagnetism has no analog in the SU(2) weak sector.

### 3.3 The Gap Remains

The literature contains no proposal for an absorber-type mechanism in the weak sector. The retarded propagator for W and Z bosons is chosen by the same universal iε prescription applied to all sectors, but this choice is not structurally justified in the way that the Wheeler-Feynman theory justifies it for photons.

## 4. The Strong CP Problem and the SU(3) Sector

### 4.1 The Theta Vacuum

QCD admits a topological term in its Lagrangian, the θ-term, which violates P and T (and hence CP, since CPT is preserved). The θ parameter multiplies a term proportional to F_μν F̃^{μν} that is a total divergence classically but contributes physically due to the non-trivial vacuum topology of SU(3).

The strong CP problem is that experimental bounds on the neutron electric dipole moment constrain |θ| < 10^{-10}, yet there is no known symmetry reason for θ to be zero. The leading proposed solution is the Peccei-Quinn mechanism, which introduces a new U(1) symmetry spontaneously broken to produce the axion, a pseudo-Goldstone boson that dynamically relaxes θ to zero.

### 4.2 Implications for Time Symmetry

The observed vanishing of θ means that the strong interaction is (to extraordinary precision) T-invariant. This has a specific implication for the time-symmetry status of QCD:

- The QCD Lagrangian (with θ ≈ 0) is time-reversal invariant at the dynamical level
- The gluon propagator still carries the universal iε prescription, selecting a causal direction
- There is no dynamical T violation to interfere with the causal phases
- The strong sector has the same "propagator selection problem" as the other sectors but no CP violation to complicate the analysis

If the axion solution is correct, then the dynamical relaxation of θ to zero actively *restores* T invariance in the strong sector. This is conceptually interesting: the Peccei-Quinn mechanism is a dynamical mechanism that eliminates a potential source of T violation, but it operates entirely at the level of the Lagrangian parameters and says nothing about the propagator boundary condition.

### 4.3 Gluon Confinement and the Absorber Problem

Gluons are confined within hadrons, with an effective range of ~1 fm. Like the massive W and Z bosons, they cannot propagate to cosmological distances. The Wheeler-Feynman absorber mechanism is even less applicable to the SU(3) sector than to SU(2): not only is the force short-range, but the force carriers themselves never appear as free particles. The retarded gluon propagator is selected by the same iε prescription, with no structural justification from an absorber condition.

## 5. CP Violation and the Cosmological Arrow of Time

### 5.1 The Sakharov Connection

CP violation plays a crucial role in cosmology through the Sakharov conditions for baryogenesis (1967). The three conditions are:
1. Baryon number violation
2. C and CP violation
3. Departure from thermal equilibrium

These conditions explain the observed matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe. CP violation is necessary so that baryons and antibaryons are produced at different rates, and the departure from equilibrium is needed so these differences are not washed out.

### 5.2 The Sakharov Connection is Not the Propagator Connection

It is tempting to see the Sakharov conditions as linking CP violation to the cosmological arrow of time, which might then connect to the propagator selection. However, the relationship is more subtle:

- The Sakharov conditions explain *baryogenesis*, not the arrow of time itself
- The departure from equilibrium (third condition) is itself a consequence of the thermodynamic arrow -- it requires that the universe was once in a special low-entropy state (the hot Big Bang) from which it expanded and cooled
- CP violation is *one ingredient* in baryogenesis, but it does not *generate* the arrow of time; it *exploits* the pre-existing thermodynamic arrow (departure from equilibrium) to produce a specific physical consequence (baryon asymmetry)

As Sean Carroll has forcefully argued (blog post, 2012; *From Eternity to Here*, 2010): "The entire phenomenon of T violation has absolutely nothing to do with [the thermodynamic] arrow of time." His reasoning:

- T-violating processes are completely **reversible** (unitary). A process and its time-reverse both occur; they simply occur at different rates.
- The arrow of time concerns **irreversibility** -- entropy increase, information loss at the macroscopic level.
- The arrow of time is explained by the low-entropy initial condition of the universe, not by any property of the microscopic dynamics.
- Even if T violation were absent (all CKM phases zero), the arrow of time from entropy increase would be identical.

### 5.3 The Boundary Condition Remains Primary

Both the propagator selection and the thermodynamic arrow trace back to boundary conditions:
- The propagator's iε prescription is equivalent to a boundary condition (positive-energy states propagate forward in time)
- The thermodynamic arrow requires the Past Hypothesis (low initial entropy)
- The Wheeler-Feynman absorber condition is explicitly cosmological

CP violation in the Lagrangian does not provide any of these boundary conditions. It is a feature of the dynamics, not of the initial or final state specifications.

## 6. Key Authors and Papers

### Foundational Works on the Propagator/Causality Arrow

- **Donoghue, J. F. and Menezes, G.**, "Arrow of Causality and Quantum Gravity," Phys. Rev. Lett. 123, 171601 (2019) [arXiv:1908.04170]. Establishes that the iε prescription encodes an arrow of causality hidden in quantization conventions.

- **Donoghue, J. F. and Menezes, G.**, "Quantum causality and the arrows of time and thermodynamics," Prog. Part. Nucl. Phys. 115, 103812 (2020) [arXiv:2003.09047]. Comprehensive treatment showing how the causal arrow generates the thermodynamic arrow, and how T-violating Lagrangian phases interfere with causal (iε) phases to produce observable CP violation.

- **Wheeler, J. A. and Feynman, R. P.**, "Interaction with the Absorber as the Mechanism of Radiation," Rev. Mod. Phys. 17, 157 (1945); "Classical Electrodynamics in Terms of Direct Interparticle Action," Rev. Mod. Phys. 21, 425 (1949). The original absorber theory for electromagnetism.

- **Cramer, J. G.**, "The Transactional Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics," Rev. Mod. Phys. 58, 647 (1986). Extension of absorber-theory ideas to quantum mechanics, using half-retarded / half-advanced solutions.

### CP Violation and T Violation

- **Christenson, J. H., Cronin, J. W., Fitch, V. L., and Turlay, R.**, "Evidence for the 2π Decay of the K_2^0 Meson," Phys. Rev. Lett. 13, 138 (1964). Discovery of CP violation.

- **Kabir, P. K.**, *The CP Puzzle: Strange Decays of the Neutral Kaon*, Academic Press (1968). Early comprehensive treatment of CP violation in the kaon system.

- **Sachs, R. G.**, *The Physics of Time Reversal*, University of Chicago Press (1987). Comprehensive treatment of T, P, and C transformations, including reformulations relevant to non-abelian gauge theories.

- **Angelopoulos, A. et al. (CPLEAR Collaboration)**, "First direct observation of time-reversal non-invariance in the neutral-kaon system," Phys. Lett. B 444, 43 (1998). First observation of T violation in kaon oscillations.

- **Lees, J. P. et al. (BaBar Collaboration)**, "Observation of Time Reversal Violation in the B^0 Meson System," Phys. Rev. Lett. 109, 211801 (2012) [arXiv:1207.5832]. First direct observation of T violation in B mesons using the Banuls-Bernabeu entanglement method.

- **Bernabeu, J., Banuls, M. C., and Martinez-Vidal, F.**, "Colloquium: Time-reversal violation with quantum-entangled B mesons," Rev. Mod. Phys. 87, 165 (2015) [arXiv:1410.1742]. Review of the theoretical framework for genuine T-violation measurements.

### T Violation vs. Arrow of Time

- **Carroll, S.**, "Time-Reversal Violation Is Not the Arrow of Time," blog post (2012); also *From Eternity to Here*, Dutton (2010). Argues forcefully that T violation is unrelated to the thermodynamic arrow; the arrow requires low-entropy initial conditions, not dynamical T violation.

- **Berger, Ch. and collaborators**, "CP violation and arrows of time: Evolution of a neutral K or B meson from an incoherent to a coherent state," Phys. Rev. D 76, 036003 (2007) [arXiv:0704.1232]. Shows that monotonic evolution of neutral meson observables defines an arrow of time constrained by CP-violating parameters, though this is a weaker sense of "arrow" than the thermodynamic one.

### Strong CP Problem

- **Peccei, R. D. and Quinn, H. R.**, "CP Conservation in the Presence of Pseudoparticles," Phys. Rev. Lett. 38, 1440 (1977). The Peccei-Quinn mechanism for resolving the strong CP problem.

- **Weinberg, S.** and **Wilczek, F.** (1978). Independent prediction of the axion as the pseudo-Goldstone boson of the broken PQ symmetry.

### Boundary Conditions and Foundations

- **Davies, P. C. W.**, *The Physics of Time Asymmetry*, University of California Press (1974). Comprehensive early treatment of time asymmetry in physics, including propagator Green functions.

- **Schulman, L. S.**, *Time's Arrows and Quantum Measurement*, Cambridge University Press (1997). Two-time boundary conditions and their implications for propagator selection.

- **Aharonov, Y., Bergmann, P. G., and Lebowitz, J. L.**, "Time Symmetry in the Quantum Process of Measurement," Phys. Rev. 134, B1410 (1964). The ABL rule for time-symmetric quantum mechanics.

## 7. Identified Gaps in the Literature

### Gap 1: No Absorber-Type Mechanism for Massive/Confined Gauge Sectors

The Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory provides a structural explanation for the retarded propagator in the U(1) electromagnetic sector. No analogous mechanism exists for:
- The SU(2) weak sector (massive W, Z bosons; range ~10^{-18} m)
- The SU(3) strong sector (confined gluons; effective range ~1 fm)

The literature does not contain any serious proposal for how an absorber condition could operate with short-range or confined force carriers. This is not merely an unsolved problem -- it appears to be an **unasked question** in most of the literature. The retarded propagator for W, Z, and gluon fields is simply imposed via the universal iε prescription without comment.

### Gap 2: The Logical Dependence is Unexplored

The observation that dynamical T violation (CP violation) presupposes the causal arrow (propagator choice) to be observable is implicit in the Donoghue-Menezes framework but is not widely recognized or systematically explored. Most treatments of CP violation take the propagator choice as given and do not inquire into its justification sector-by-sector.

### Gap 3: The Relationship Between the Causal Arrow and the Thermodynamic Arrow

Donoghue and Menezes argue that the causal arrow generates the thermodynamic arrow: elementary processes flowing in one direction produce statistical disorder. This claim, if correct, would mean the propagator selection problem is not merely a formal issue but is foundational for all macroscopic time asymmetry. However, this view is not universally accepted. Carroll and others maintain that the thermodynamic arrow requires a low-entropy initial condition (the Past Hypothesis) and cannot be derived from the microscopic causal structure alone.

### Gap 4: The Strong CP Problem as a Time-Symmetry Restoration

The Peccei-Quinn mechanism dynamically drives θ to zero, restoring T invariance in QCD. No one has investigated whether this dynamical T-restoration has implications for the propagator selection problem in the SU(3) sector -- for example, whether the absence of dynamical T violation in QCD makes the propagator selection problem in that sector "purer" or more tractable.

### Gap 5: CP Violation in the Neutrino Sector

The PMNS matrix for neutrino mixing may contain a CP-violating phase (current experiments like DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande are investigating). Neutrinos, being extremely light and weakly interacting, propagate over cosmological distances -- unlike W/Z bosons. If CP violation is confirmed in the neutrino sector, the question of whether this has any bearing on the propagator selection for the SU(2) gauge fields (which mediate neutrino interactions) could be worth investigating, though no literature currently addresses this.

## 8. Conclusion: Clear Answer to the Assigned Question

**The dynamical T violation from CP violation in the weak sector and the conventional time asymmetry in the propagator choice are logically distinct but physically entangled.**

They are distinct because:
- The propagator's causal arrow is a **kinematic/boundary condition** feature, encoded in the iε prescription, universal across all gauge sectors, and independent of whether the Lagrangian violates CP.
- CP violation is a **dynamical** feature, specific to the weak sector (and possibly the neutrino sector), arising from the complex CKM phase.

They are entangled because:
- Observable CP violation requires interference between weak phases (from the CKM matrix) and strong phases (from the iε prescription / final-state interactions). Without the causal arrow, CP violation would be unobservable.
- The causal arrow provides the "stage" on which dynamical T violation performs. The CKM phase distinguishes particles from antiparticles in time, but only against the background of a pre-existing temporal direction.

CP violation does **not** do the structural work of the absorber condition:
- It cannot explain the propagator selection because it presupposes it.
- The physical mechanism (short-range massive gauge bosons) prevents any absorber-type mechanism from operating.
- It is a property of the laws, not of the boundary conditions.

The propagator selection problem in the weak and strong sectors remains structurally unjustified. Only the electromagnetic sector has a candidate explanation (the Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory), and even that remains debated. The existence of CP violation in the weak sector is an interesting dynamical fact about the Standard Model, but it is orthogonal to the foundational question of why the retarded propagator is chosen in any gauge sector.
