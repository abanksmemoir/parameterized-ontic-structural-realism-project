# Methodological Lessons

**What I learned from Mike's corrections during the V2 session. If you're a future agent, these will save you the same mistakes.**

---

## Lesson 1: Don't subordinate the project to a use case

My first instinct was to frame the POSR graph as "infrastructure for navigating theoretical commitments that bear on a specific experimental question." Mike corrected this immediately: "It is an exercise in natural philosophy." The graph isn't built to serve the NMK experiment or any other specific application. It's built to be correct. If it's correct, it'll also be useful — but usefulness is a consequence, not a design goal.

**What this means in practice:** Don't structure nodes, annotations, or metadata to optimize for answering a specific question. Don't add fields because they'd be helpful for simulation. Don't reorganize layers because it would make the experimental connection clearer. Make the graph accurate and let it speak for itself.

## Lesson 2: "Everything is a parameter" is the ontological commitment

When I tried to put the iε prescription in `presupposes` rather than `parametric`, Mike pushed back. My reasoning was: it's a single global choice enforced by unitarity, so it's structural, not parametric. His reasoning was: "parametric" means you can contemplate alternatives and game them out from common roots. The −iε theory is well-formed. Both values are internally consistent. That makes it a parameter, regardless of whether the physical universe instantiates only one value.

This extends to everything. "Presupposes" in the v1 graph was masking theory-construction parameters behind an appearance of logical necessity. The audit (D2 in V2_NOTE.md) found 7 conventional dependencies out of ~50 — cases where what looked like a requirement was actually a choice of substrate.

**What this means in practice:** When you encounter something that looks like a fixed commitment, ask: "Is the alternative well-formed?" If yes, it's a parameter. The only things that aren't parameters are logical entailments — things where the alternative is ill-formed, not just unusual.

## Lesson 3: Direction of justification matters

I said the iε prescription "lives at the boundary of PATH and QFT" because it connects to propagators (PATH) and field quantization (QFT) and has downstream consequences for causality and energy exchange. Mike corrected the direction: the iε prescription is *logically prior* to those consequences. It sits closer to the root because higher-level structures presuppose it. The fact that it has big payoffs at higher layers is evidence of its importance, but its *position* in the graph is determined by what it presupposes, not by what it enables.

**What this means in practice:** When placing something in the graph, ask "what does it need to exist?" not "what does it make possible?" The dependency arrows point from what's needed to what needs it. Don't put a node high in the graph just because it has many consequences — put it where its own preconditions place it.

## Lesson 4: Time symmetry is "pushed upward" with contingent containment

Mike's key phrase: the time-symmetric dynamics are "pushed upward into the real world with the expectation that the consequences are containable due to contingent factors." The Yang-Mills equations are time-symmetric. That symmetry propagates up through the graph. At each layer, contingent factors (absorber conditions, confinement, mass generation, cosmological expansion) *contain* the consequences — making it look like time has a preferred direction. But the containment is contingent, not structural. The NMK/Bajlo regime is one place where the containment fails and the underlying symmetry leaks through.

**What this means in practice:** When annotating nodes, don't say "this node provides the retarded propagator." Say "this node provides the retarded propagator *contingent on* boundary conditions that may fail locally." The graph should make the contingency visible, not hide it behind a provision that reads as unconditional.

## Lesson 5: Register the variable, don't resolve it

When I proposed resolving the open questions (extending to cosmology, disambiguating "structural," connecting temporal parameters), Mike's response was consistent: note that the parameter takes a value, note what value practice assumes, note that the justification is absent or incomplete, flag it for research. Don't resolve it in the graph.

This applies to the vacuum state (OQ2), the Gribov region (OQ3), the generation count (OQ4), and the temporal parameter entanglement (OQ5). In every case, the right move is the same: register, don't resolve.

**What this means in practice:** Resist the urge to close open questions. The graph's value partly lies in making the open questions visible. A graph that pretends everything is settled is less useful than one that honestly maps where the settlements are and where they aren't.

## Lesson 6: Mike corrects framing, not facts

In this session, Mike never said "that fact is wrong." Every correction was about framing — how I characterized something, what I implied about its role, what category I put it in. The facts (what the literature says, what the math does) were fine. The interpretive layer was where I kept drifting.

The most common drift: I kept making the graph *about something* (about the experiment, about resolving the debt, about extending to cosmology). Mike kept pulling it back to: the graph is a map. Make the map accurate. Let the reader decide what it's about.

**What this means in practice:** State what is. Don't editorialize about significance. Don't frame nodes in terms of what problem they solve. Describe what they are, what they provide, and what they assume.
