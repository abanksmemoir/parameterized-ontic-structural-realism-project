# Origin Story

**This is the passage Mike shared during our session that explains why the POSR project exists. It is not recorded in any other file in the repo. If you are a future agent and you haven't read this, you don't understand the project.**

---

When Feynman wrote his PhD thesis (1942, under Wheeler), he didn't start with the version of electrodynamics everyone uses. He started with a stranger, more symmetrical version: one where electromagnetic waves travel both forward *and* backward in time. The standard approach — which every RF engineer, antenna designer, and telecom modeler uses — quietly throws away the backward-traveling solutions on day one. You pick "retarded" boundary conditions (waves radiate outward from the source, forward in time), and you never look back. It works. It's technically a choice, though, not a derivation.

Feynman showed that if you *don't* make that choice — if you keep both the forward ("retarded") and backward ("advanced") waves and instead let the boundary conditions do the work — you recover all of standard electrodynamics *plus* you get the radiation reaction for free, without the infinities that plague the standard approach. And to be clear: in 99.999% of cases, everyone is right to ignore this. The retarded-only model works. Feynman earned his PhD in part by giving a rigorous cosmological argument for *why* it works — the universe, under certain absorber conditions, conspires to make the advanced contributions cancel out, leaving you with exactly the physics you'd have gotten by throwing them away at the start.

But Feynman didn't forget about it. When he went on to develop the path integral formulation of quantum mechanics — and with it, Feynman diagrams, the framework that animates everything people are doing at CERN, the toolkit that lets physicists "do the equations and not think about it" — he built a structure that implicitly contemplates, at least instrumentally, the idea that the future can affect the past. The propagators in those diagrams run both directions in time. Antiparticles are formally described as particles moving backward through time. The entire machinery works because it doesn't enforce the one-way-causation assumption that classical electrodynamics adopted as a convenience. Feynman kept the symmetry in his back pocket and wove it into the foundations of modern particle physics.

The theory never quite disappeared. John Cramer, an emeritus professor of physics at the University of Washington, spent a good stretch of his career publishing on exactly this — what he called the Transactional Interpretation of quantum mechanics, which takes the advanced-wave framework seriously as more than bookkeeping. Cramer is mainstream, credentialed, not a crank. But his work never got much experimental traction. Not a lot of people are rushing to play around in a dusty and frankly weird corner of physics, and nobody seems especially interested in funding it.

Then something shifted on the theoretical side. In 2015, Niknejadi, Madey, and Kowalczyk published a paper in *Physical Review D* — a mainstream, refereed journal — addressing a nagging problem: conventional electrodynamics was failing to accurately predict the radiation behavior of coherently oscillating electrons in a free-electron laser, particularly when radiating into free space. Their fix was to revive the Wheeler-Feynman assumption that an oscillating charge radiates advanced and retarded waves with equal amplitudes. It worked. It resolved problems the standard framework couldn't. And it came with a specific experimental proposal: redo an earlier null-result experiment from 1980, but with a much smaller downstream antenna — one reduced to 1/20th of a wavelength, small enough that it wouldn't fully absorb the incoming signal, which NMK's calculations predicted should leave the advanced-wave component uncancelled and therefore detectable.

Darko Bajlo — a retired Croatian military electronic-surveillance specialist, tinkering in his back yard — picked up the NMK design and ran with it. Between December 2016 and January 2017, he performed 500 runs using pulsed radio waves and a 1/20th-wavelength monopole antenna downstream from his transmitter. The results weren't marginal: he observed advanced signals arriving at the downstream antenna *before the transmitter fired*, at 7 to 26+ standard deviations above noise. The signals behaved the way the theory predicted — amplitude decreased when he lengthened the antenna, increased the downstream distance, or lowered the angle toward the horizon (increasing atmospheric absorption along the path). He even observed amplitude variation as the Earth rotated and the antenna axis swept across the galactic center, where absorption should be higher.

But the results are genuinely ambiguous in the way that matters. Bajlo's data was published as a preprint, not in a refereed journal. He didn't have institutional lab conditions. One US investigator with good equipment tried to replicate and couldn't. Julius Madey — whose late brother John was a co-author on the NMK paper — says he *did* corroborate Bajlo's results with his own apparatus, but those results are also unpublished. The formal replication bar hasn't been cleared.

Mike doesn't know why none of this is getting published. But there's a computational route into the problem that doesn't require a lab, funding, or institutional backing. The equations underlying both the retarded-only and the time-symmetric (advanced + retarded) frameworks are well-behaved and well-documented. And we're now at a point where AI coding agents are capable of iteratively building and refining electromagnetic simulations from that literature.

**That's what this project is ultimately about.** Building a modeling environment that covers exactly this territory — simulating different absorber geometries, antenna configurations, boundary conditions, and atmospheric parameters to see what kinds of experimental setups should generate unambiguous or at least clearly testable conditions for determining whether the NMK predictions are correct, whether Cramer's framework holds up, and whether Bajlo detected something real. A computational sandbox for designing better experiments in a neglected corner of physics where the theory is rigorous, the experimental record is sparse and contested, and nobody's bothered to build the tools that would let you figure out where to look next.

**The dependency graph is the map.** It tells you what you're committing to when you make a choice at any layer, what breaks if you change it, and where the assumed-but-unjustified choices live. The iε prescription (P057) is the fulcrum: the standard choice makes the advanced waves go away; the time-symmetric alternative keeps them. The graph's job is to make that choice, and everything that depends on it, fully legible.
